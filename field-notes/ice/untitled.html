ICE

A night of chants, sirens, and the uneasy choreography of power. Not spectacle — evidence. Not “content” — a record.
Some nights feel like a long exposure: the city bright, the edges vibrating, everything moving but nothing changing fast enough.

I brought a camera because I didn’t trust my memory to stay intact. I wanted the frames that don’t scream — the ones that prove you were there. Hands up. Faces lit by phone screens. A poster creased like it had already survived.

If these images feel uncomfortable, good. They should. Comfort is not the point.

What I noticed

The loudest parts were easy: the chants, the cars, the megaphones. The quieter parts were the ones that stayed: people checking on strangers, a hand on a shoulder, someone offering water like it was a moral reflex.

Photography is a blunt instrument. You point it at something and you’re saying: this mattered enough to keep.

I’m not interested in turning protest into aesthetic. But I’m also not going to pretend the city doesn’t have a visual language. It does. And on nights like this, it’s written in hard light, shadows, and the way people hold their bodies when they’re ready to run.

In the middle of it

The camera doesn’t protect you. It just gives your hands something to do while your brain tries to understand what it’s seeing.

Why keep this

Because “I was there” turns into “I think I remember” turns into nothing. These frames are a receipt. For anger. For hope. For the people who showed up when it would’ve been easier not to.
